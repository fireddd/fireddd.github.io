Before payments, I spent a year or so as a quantitative strategist at Morgan Stanley, on a desk in the Fixed Income Division most people outside a bank have never heard of: Macro Valuation Adjustments. This is what that work is, and what I built there.

What a valuation adjustment is

A bank's balance sheet carries a number nobody trades and everybody watches: the capital it has to set aside to satisfy its regulators. A valuation adjustment is a piece of that — a reserve held against the firm's positions so that its books, and its capital, line up with what the rules require.

What makes it interesting is that it cuts both ways. Set it too low and you are in breach — you have not reserved enough, and the regulator has a problem with you. Set it too high and you have parked capital that could have been earning; every dollar over-reserved carries an opportunity cost. So the job is not "make the number big and safe." It is to make it exactly right — just enough to satisfy the rules, and not a dollar more.

Why it's hard

Two things make that hard. The first is that the rules are not one set of rules. Different markets regulate differently, so the same adjustment has to be computed differently depending on where the desk sits — a customisation per trading location, not a single formula.

The second is that, historically, a lot of this was done by hand. And hand-computed regulatory numbers go wrong. A mistake here is not a P&L error you book and move on from; it is a compliance failure, with the regulatory and reputational consequences that come with one. That is the whole reason this work matters: the cost of the number being wrong is measured in something far more expensive than money.

What I built

I was on the Macro Valuation Adjustment team, and my job was to take these off manual footing — on an in-house, Scala-based graph-execution framework, with internal libraries layered on top. The shape of every one of them was the same once you saw it: read some data, run a set of complex calculations over it, and push the results into the downstream systems that book and report them. The plumbing was never the hard part — the methodologies were, the actual math of each adjustment, which differed by product and by desk.

I built and supported more than ten of these, across the FX desk in Brazil and the interest-rate desks in London, Hong Kong and New York — each with its own method and its own jurisdiction's rules.

Automating them meant more than "run the calc." It meant a tool the people who owned these numbers could actually operate: run an adjustment from a UI, validate the data before anything was pushed — so a bad input was caught before it became a bad regulatory number — and fire off the relevant figures by email to the wider set of stakeholders who needed them. The validation is the part that mattered most: the whole point was a number people could trust, and you do not get trust from a calculation nobody checked.

The payoff

Tighter controls, and roughly 25 engineer-hours a month handed back to the Valuation Control and Strats teams. But the hours saved were the least of it. The real win was moving a regulatory number off a manual process that could quietly go wrong and onto one that validated its own inputs and left a trail anyone could follow.

In a bank, the unglamorous reliability of a number the regulator trusts is worth more than the time it saves. It is the same lesson every job since has taught me again: the interesting work is rarely the calculation — it is making the calculation something you can stake a firm on.
