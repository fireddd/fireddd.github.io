The drill you can pass and still be wrong

phonepe · disaster recovery · omx

In regulated payments you don't get to claim you can fail over. You have to demonstrate it. India's business-continuity expectations for payment systems mean a disaster-recovery drill is a recurring, audited exercise — not a document someone signs once and files.

I led the DR for OMX, one of our online merchant services. When these systems are down, it isn't an internal inconvenience — it's merchants unable to collect money.

Learning infrastructure I didn't own

I owned the application, not the plumbing. To lead the drill credibly I had to understand the plumbing anyway: cross-datacentre replication, how the API gateway routes, how service discovery resolves across data centres, and what a drill actually consists of — the steps, the end state, and the ways it fails.

I read the DR documentation end to end and sat in on other teams' knowledge-transfer sessions to see how their failovers really behaved. That ramp is the only reason I caught what came next. I would never have seen it from the application layer.

The plan would have passed

The proposed failover plan worked like this: leave the API gateway where it was, and let a deployed sidecar redirect traffic to the surviving instance.

That plan passes the drill. In a drill, you take one service down and prove traffic reaches it somewhere else. The sidecar redirect does exactly that.

But I kept pulling on what a real disaster looks like, and a real disaster is not one service dying. It's the entire data centre going down. Under that condition, the path we were about to certify doesn't hold — the very layer being relied on to redirect traffic is inside the thing that just died.

So we would have passed. We would have shown the auditors a green result, filed it, and walked away with false confidence — which is worse than no confidence, because you stop looking.

Making the expensive call

I said so. The correct approach was the one that survives losing the whole data centre, not the one that survives the test — and it cost us timeline to do it that way.

Two things mattered about how I raised it. First, I didn't argue it as a preference. I argued it as a failure mode: here is the condition under which our plan stops working, and that condition is the actual definition of the disaster we're preparing for. That's much harder to wave through than "I'd prefer the other design."

Second, the gap wasn't in the DR document at all — the standard operating procedure didn't require the right path. So fixing my drill would have fixed nothing for anyone else. I got the SOP changed, so the next team doesn't rediscover a full-datacentre loss the hard way. That's the part that outlives me.

The numbers

We completed the drill on the correct path, with a measured recovery time. RTO came down from about an hour to under seven minutes, and it was still heading downwards — a lot of the cutover was manual and we were automating it.

The thing people get wrong about RPO is treating it as a target you choose. It isn't. Your RPO is set for you by the slowest-replicating store you depend on. You can write whatever number you like in the document; the database's asynchronous replication lag is the number that will actually be true on the day. Everything else — the caches, the queues whose state you can rebuild from the database — is noise by comparison.

The part I got wrong

I committed to a December date. It slipped twice, about two weeks each time.

The cause was that OMX was the first service to require its upstream and downstream callers to reach it a new way, and a couple of those teams hadn't migrated in time. I had started chasing them a month earlier — and that's exactly the problem. I treated "I've asked" as "they'll do it."

They didn't report to me, but that isn't an excuse; it's the whole job. Now I track dependency migrations as line items on my own plan, and I chase them as if they were my deliverable — because they are.

What I'd tell you it taught me

A drill is a test, and tests can be gamed — usually by accident, by people who are trying hard and want to pass. The most dangerous thing in reliability work isn't a failure. It's a green result you didn't earn.

If your recovery plan is only exercised against a failure smaller than the one you're actually afraid of, you don't have a recovery plan. You have a rehearsal.
