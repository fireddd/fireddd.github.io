The drill you can pass and still be wrong

I led the DR for OMX. The plan would have passed the drill and failed the disaster.

  • Cross-DC replication
  • API gateway routing
  • Service discovery
  • Active–passive
  • RTO 1 hr → <7 min

In regulated payments you don't get to claim you can fail over. You have to demonstrate it. India's business-continuity expectations for payment systems mean a disaster-recovery drill is a recurring, audited exercise — not a document someone signs once and files.

I have taken part in several DR drills at PhonePe, and I led the one for OMX, one of our online merchant services. When these systems are down it isn't an internal inconvenience — it's merchants unable to collect money.

I owned the application, not the plumbing. To lead a drill credibly I had to learn the plumbing anyway. This is what I learnt, roughly in the order you need it.

Active-passive, RTO and RPO

Start with the shape. We ran active-passive: two data centres, but only one of them takes writes at a time. The other is a warm standby, kept up to date by replication, doing nothing until the day it has to do everything.

The alternative — active-active, both sites taking writes — sounds better and is much harder. The moment two data centres can both accept a write for the same account, you own a conflict resolution problem, and in payments the conflicts are somebody's money. Active-passive trades that away and buys you a much simpler failure story: everything is over there now.

Two numbers describe how good your failover is, and they measure completely different things.

  • RTO — recovery time objective. How long you are down. From the moment the primary is lost to the moment traffic is being served from the secondary. This is a time, and it is mostly a function of how much of your cutover is still manual.
  • RPO — recovery point objective. How much data you lose. If the primary vanishes right now, how far behind is the standby? This is not a time you get to choose. It is handed to you by your slowest-replicating datastore, and no amount of planning changes it.

That distinction is the one people get wrong. RTO is an engineering problem — automate the steps and it comes down. RPO is a property of your storage. You can write any number you like in the document; what will actually be true on the day is your database's replication lag.

What we ran, and how it replicates

Every component in the stack fails over differently, and your RPO is the worst of them.

  • MariaDB — the transactional store, and the one that matters. Active-passive with asynchronous replication, lagging the primary by a couple of minutes. This is the binding constraint. MariaDB's replication lag was our RPO; nothing else in the stack moved that number.
  • Aerospike — run in AP mode with bidirectional cross-datacentre replication in the low hundreds of milliseconds. Effectively real time. Its contribution to data loss is negligible.
  • RabbitMQ — asynchronous processing only. The durable state lives in the database, so messages in flight are reconstructable from persisted state. RMQ is not a data-loss surface on its own, which is a nice property to have designed in before you need it.
  • ZooKeeper — coordination and leader election. Also what Ranger, the open-source service-discovery library, stores its service registry in.
  • Drove and drove-gateway — container orchestration, and the NGINX layer that routes traffic to wherever the containers actually are. Both are open source.

So the honest RPO was about two minutes, set entirely by MariaDB. Aerospike and RabbitMQ don't get a vote. If you want a better RPO you do not buy a better runbook — you change how the database replicates.

The steps of a drill

What actually happens, in order:

  1. Stop the writes. Engage the API killer at the gateway so nothing new enters the primary. Until writes stop, the standby is chasing a moving target.
  2. Let replication settle. Wait for the standby to catch up, and only then declare its database state authoritative. Skip this and you have just chosen your data loss instead of measuring it.
  3. Move service discovery. Callers have to be able to find the service in the other data centre. This is where the interesting failure lives, and it's the subject of the rest of this post.
  4. Switch traffic at the gateway. Repoint the routing layer at the secondary.
  5. Bring the service up, and prove it. Not "the containers are running" — real traffic, served correctly, verified.

A lot of that was still manual when I inherited it, and manual steps are where RTO goes to die. Automating them is how the number came down.

The plan would have passed

Step three — moving service discovery — is where the whole thing turned.

The proposed failover plan worked like this: leave the routing layer where it was, and let a sidecar deployed inside the primary data centre redirect traffic to the surviving instance.

That plan passes the drill. In a drill, you take one service down and prove traffic reaches it somewhere else. The sidecar redirect does exactly that.

But I kept pulling on what a real disaster looks like, and a real disaster is not one service dying. It's the entire data centre going down. Under that condition, the path we were about to certify doesn't hold — the very layer being relied on to redirect traffic is inside the thing that just died.

So we would have passed. We would have shown the auditors a green result, filed it, and walked away with false confidence — which is worse than no confidence, because you stop looking.

Making the expensive call

I said so. The correct approach was the one that survives losing the whole data centre, not the one that survives the test — and it cost us timeline to do it that way.

Two things mattered about how I raised it. First, I didn't argue it as a preference. I argued it as a failure mode: here is the condition under which our plan stops working, and that condition is the actual definition of the disaster we're preparing for. That's much harder to wave through than "I'd prefer the other design."

Second, the gap wasn't in the DR document at all — the standard operating procedure didn't require the right path. So fixing my drill would have fixed nothing for anyone else. I got the SOP changed, so the next team doesn't rediscover a full-datacentre loss the hard way. That's the part that outlives me.

What a drill actually looks like

A drill is not a diagram. It is a room, and it is the middle of the night, because that is the only window in which you are allowed to take a payment system down on purpose.

Before any of it, somebody has to go round and ask everyone whether they are actually turning up. A colleague made this, and it is the most accurate artefact of the whole exercise:

On the call: engineers, an architect, SREs and their manager, compliance, and external auditors. Roughly a dozen people, most of whom do not report to you, all watching a clock. That is the part the runbook does not prepare you for — the failover is a technical problem, and everything around it is not.

The numbers

We completed the drill on the correct path, with a measured recovery time. RTO came down from about an hour to under seven minutes, and it was still heading downwards — a lot of the cutover was manual and we were automating it.

The thing people get wrong about RPO is treating it as a target you choose. It isn't. Your RPO is set for you by the slowest-replicating store you depend on. You can write whatever number you like in the document; the database's asynchronous replication lag is the number that will actually be true on the day. Everything else — the caches, the queues whose state you can rebuild from the database — is noise by comparison.

The part I got wrong

I committed to a December date. It slipped twice, about two weeks each time.

The cause was that OMX was the first service to require its upstream and downstream callers to reach it a new way, and a couple of those teams hadn't migrated in time. I had started chasing them a month earlier — and that's exactly the problem. I treated "I've asked" as "they'll do it."

They didn't report to me, but that isn't an excuse; it's the whole job. Now I track dependency migrations as line items on my own plan, and I chase them as if they were my deliverable — because they are.

What I'd tell you it taught me

A drill is a test, and tests can be gamed — usually by accident, by people who are trying hard and want to pass. The most dangerous thing in reliability work isn't a failure. It's a green result you didn't earn.

If your recovery plan is only exercised against a failure smaller than the one you're actually afraid of, you don't have a recovery plan. You have a rehearsal.
