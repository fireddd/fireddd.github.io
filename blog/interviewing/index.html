Take every interview they'll let you take

Taking interviews is treated as a tax on your time. It was the highest-leverage, least glamorous thing I did for my own career — and it ran on one golden rule.

  • Machine coding
  • DSA problem solving
  • System design
  • Campus drives
  • 50+ rounds

At a big company, taking interviews is treated as a tax. It lands on the senior engineers, it eats an afternoon, and the reward for doing it well is that you are asked to do more of it. I came to see it the other way round: volunteering to interview was the highest-leverage, least glamorous investment I made in my own career at PhonePe.

None of what it paid back is on your team's roadmap — which is exactly why it is easy to skip. Here is what it actually bought.

The rounds I took

Over my time there I ran more than thirty DSA problem-solving rounds and more than ten machine-coding rounds, and shadowed more than ten design rounds. The DSA round watches how a person thinks when they are stuck; the machine-coding round watches them build something small and working against the clock — which, between them, is most of what you can honestly learn about someone in an hour.

I was cleared to run the design round on my own. I chose to shadow first — sitting in on the senior engineers who ran it, saying little, watching how they worked.

That is where the learning was. Watching someone senior probe a design teaches you what a good one sounds like: what they push on and what they let slide, how they turn a candidate's answer into the next question, when they stop drilling because they already have their signal. You learn the round from the far side of the table, and it makes you sharper on either side of it — the one asking, and the one being asked.

The campus drives

The drives took me across the country — SPIT, IIT Kharagpur, IIT Hyderabad, PEC Chandigarh, NIT Surathkal, IIIT Allahabad, with online rounds for others, like Cummins College. The travel did something my own desk never could. It put me in a room with engineers from teams I would otherwise never cross, running rounds beside them — and it let me see the kind of work those teams actually do. Not the one-line version on an org chart; the real thing, from the people doing it.

By the end, the recruiter running the drives knew me by name. I had turned up that many times.

The golden rule

Campus hiring at PhonePe had a high bar, and it set a pace I would never have set myself. Preparation that would comfortably take a week had to be done in three or four hours. The night before a drive I would be up until two or three in the morning — not revising trivia, but formulating every approach a candidate might reasonably bring to a problem, and working out where each one broke.

That preparation exists to serve one rule, the rule I ran every round by: if you cannot refute a candidate's approach, you have to accept it. You do not get to reject an answer because it is not the one you would have written. You reject it only if you can point to a concrete flaw — and if you cannot, it is valid, and you say so. It keeps you honest, and it is unforgiving of your own preparation: to judge an approach fairly on the spot, you have to have already thought it through yourself, the night before, at 2am.

It is also what makes the round land for the person on the other side. A candidate once told me, at the end of a round, that it was the best interview they had given in their life — and you do not get that by grilling anyone. You get it by having done the work, so that the round is hard and fair at once, and the candidate can feel both.

What it paid back

Three things, and none of them is the reason you get asked to interview in the first place.

  • A network that unblocks you. When you have met someone in person, they reply faster. A cross-team dependency that would otherwise sit in a queue for days moves in an hour, because you know the person on the other end and they know you.
  • The gossip nobody puts online. Interviewing across teams, and meeting the people on them, got me the unwritten version of the company — the candid, word-of-mouth read on what each team is really like to work on, the day-to-day that never makes it onto a review site or an org chart. It is exactly the intelligence you wish you had before you choose where to sit.
  • Exposure to the whole company. This is the one I value most. You come away understanding the place far past your own service. Most engineers spend a career inside one team and never once see how the rest of it is built; interviewing drags you across all of it.

What I'd tell you

  • Say yes to interviewing. The craft, the network and the exposure compound, and none of it competes with your real work for roadmap space — it simply is not on the roadmap at all.
  • Shadow before you solo. Being cleared to run a round is not the same as knowing how to run one well; the fastest way to learn is to sit beside someone who already does, before you ever run it alone.
  • Out-prepare the room. The golden rule — refute it or accept it — only holds if you have already thought through every approach before the candidate sits down. The 2am preparation is not overkill; it is the job.

None of this was the job. All of it made me better at the job — and better at knowing which job to want next.
